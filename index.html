Galaxies are vast cosmic systems composed of stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas, dust, dark matter, and more, all bound together by gravity. Our own Milky Way is just one of billions of galaxies in the observable universe.

What is a Galaxy?

A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system consisting of stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas, dust, dark matter, and possibly unseen forms of matter. The word galaxy is derived from the Greek word for "milky," a reference to our own galaxy, the Milky Way.

Did you know? The Milky Way contains between 100-400 billion stars and is approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter.

Galaxies range in size from dwarfs with just a few hundred million stars to giants with one hundred trillion stars, each orbiting its galaxy's center of mass. Most galaxies are organized into distinct shapes that allow for classification: spiral, elliptical, and irregular.

Types of Galaxies

Spiral Galaxies

Spiral galaxies consist of a flat, rotating disk of stars, gas, and dust, along with a central concentration of stars known as the bulge. These are surrounded by a much fainter halo of stars, many of which reside in globular clusters.

Examples: Milky Way, Andromeda Galaxy (M31)

Elliptical Galaxies

Elliptical galaxies have smooth, featureless light distributions and appear as ellipses in images. They are generally composed of older, redder stars, and contain little interstellar matter. They vary greatly in both size and mass.

Examples: M87, M49

Irregular Galaxies

Irregular galaxies have no definite structure or shape. They are often chaotic in appearance, with neither spiral nor elliptical features. Many irregular galaxies were once spiral or elliptical galaxies that were deformed by gravitational interaction.

Examples: Large Magellanic Cloud, Small Magellanic Cloud

Components of Galaxies

Stars

Stars are the most visible component of galaxies. They vary in size, mass, temperature, and age. Stars are born in nebulae, live for millions to billions of years, and eventually die, sometimes in spectacular explosions called supernovae.

Interstellar Medium

The space between stars is not empty but filled with gas and dust, collectively known as the interstellar medium (ISM). This material is crucial for star formation and makes up about 10-15% of the visible matter in our galaxy.

Dark Matter

Dark matter is an invisible form of matter that doesn't interact with the electromagnetic force but would still have gravitational effects on ordinary matter. Dark matter makes up about 85% of the total matter in the universe and about 27% of its total mass-energy.

Cosmic Mystery: Despite making up most of a galaxy's mass, dark matter has never been directly observed and is one of the biggest mysteries in modern astrophysics.

Black Holes

Most, if not all, large galaxies contain a supermassive black hole at their center. These black holes can have masses millions or billions of times that of our Sun. The Milky Way's central black hole, Sagittarius A*, has a mass of about 4 million solar masses.

Globular Clusters

Globular clusters are spherical collections of stars that orbit a galactic core. They are very tightly bound by gravity, giving them their spherical shapes and relatively high stellar densities toward their centers.

Galaxy Formation and Evolution

Galaxies form and evolve through a complex interplay of gravity, dark matter, and the physics of gas dynamics. The current model suggests that galaxies began as small density fluctuations in the early universe, which grew through gravitational attraction, drawing in gas that eventually formed stars.

Galaxies continue to evolve through:

  • Mergers: When galaxies collide and combine
  • Star formation: Creating new stars from interstellar gas
  • Supernovae: Explosive deaths of massive stars that enrich the galaxy with heavy elements
  • Active galactic nuclei: Periods when the central black hole actively consumes matter

Future Event: Our Milky Way is on a collision course with the Andromeda Galaxy. In about 4.5 billion years, the two galaxies will merge to form a new, larger galaxy sometimes referred to as "Milkomeda."
